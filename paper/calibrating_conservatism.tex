\begin{abstract}
Conservative Q-Learning applies a fixed, globally uniform penalty to value
estimates regardless of local data coverage. We study whether that penalty is
spatially miscalibrated on the path-concentrated benchmark
\texttt{maze2d-medium-sparse-v1}, comparing seven offline RL methods
(TD3-BC, CQL, Q-ensemble, BEAR, UWAC, MOPO, APTQ-CQL) under a matched
PyTorch protocol with Bellman backups, target critics, and three random
seeds. Methods are ranked by a unified offline score---mean
$Q(s,\pi(s))$ on held-out dataset states---and further analysed with
coverage-tier Conservative Excess Ratio (CER) and Uncertainty--Return
Correlation (URC) diagnostics. CQL attains the highest proxy score
($2.29 \pm 0.14$), followed by APTQ-CQL ($1.33 \pm 0.14$). Tier-wise CER
shows CQL is uniformly \emph{less} conservative than a Monte Carlo value
baseline ($\mathrm{CER}{<}0$ in all tiers), with the largest gap in
low-coverage regions; APTQ-CQL reduces this excess, especially along
well-covered paths ($\mathrm{CER}\approx -3.1$ vs.\ $-14.5$ in the high
tier). URC is positive in every tier for both methods ($+0.48$ to $+0.54$),
ensemble uncertainty does not anticorrelate with return on this dataset.
These findings motivate coverage-conditioned penalties $\alpha(s){=}f(\rho(s))$
rather than a single global $\alpha$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Offline RL enables policy learning from static, pre-collected datasets
without further environment interaction~\cite{levine2020offline}. This
setting is critical for safety-constrained domains such as clinical
decision support, autonomous driving, and robotic navigation, where online
exploration is costly or ethically untenable. The core algorithmic challenge
is distributional shift: a learned policy may visit state--action pairs
underrepresented in the training data, yielding overestimated Q-values for
out-of-distribution actions~\cite{fujimoto2019bcq}.

Pessimism under uncertainty has emerged as the dominant principle for
addressing distributional shift. CQL~\cite{kumar2020conservative} penalises
Q-values of OOD actions by augmenting the Bellman objective with a
regularisation term controlled by a fixed coefficient $\alpha$. Follow-up
work has strengthened these foundations through ensemble
disagreement~\cite{an2021pbrl,wu2021uncertainty} and adaptive penalty
weighting~\cite{liu2023aptq}.

The majority of the theoretical and algorithmic literature evaluates on
locomotion benchmarks---halfcheetah, hopper, and walker2d---which share
dense, near-continuous state--action coverage from behaviourally smooth
trajectory distributions~\cite{fu2020d4rl}. The implicit assumption that a
single fixed penalty $\alpha$ can reliably safeguard the entire state--action
space conceals a deeper miscalibration problem that becomes structurally
visible in qualitatively different navigation domains.

In sparse-reward 2D navigation, coverage is topographically concentrated
along traversed paths, with large regions entirely absent from the dataset.
Uncertainty is spatially structured: near-zero along demonstrated
trajectories, extremely high in unexplored regions. A fixed global $\alpha$
therefore simultaneously over-penalises well-supported path regions and
under-penalises truly sparse ones.

The contributions of this paper are as follows.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item A unified empirical comparison of seven offline RL baselines on
    \texttt{maze2d-medium-sparse-v1}---TD3-BC, CQL, an uncertainty-aware
    Q-ensemble, BEAR, UWAC, MOPO, and APTQ-CQL---trained and evaluated under
    matched architectures, optimisers, and evaluation windows across three
    random seeds.
  \item Coverage-tier diagnostics (CER and URC) computed on
    \texttt{maze2d-medium-sparse-v1}, testing whether conservatism excess
    tracks epistemic uncertainty differently across high-, medium-, and
    low-coverage regions of the dataset.
  \item A reproducible experimental protocol---shared architectures,
    Bellman backups with target networks, fixed training budget, and a
    single evaluation metric---documented in code with released artifacts.
\end{itemize}

Section~\ref{sec:background} formalises the problem setting and reviews
related work. Section~\ref{sec:method} describes the empirical framework
and diagnostic metrics as designed. Section~\ref{sec:setup} details the
experimental setup. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the updated results.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications and outlines the
coverage-conditioned extension, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Markov Decision Processes and Offline RL}

The problem is formulated as an infinite-horizon Markov Decision Process
$\mathcal{M} = (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, r, \gamma)$, where
$\mathcal{S}$ is the state space, $\mathcal{A}$ is the action space,
$P: \mathcal{S}\times\mathcal{A} \to \Delta(\mathcal{S})$ is the transition
kernel, $r: \mathcal{S}\times\mathcal{A}\to\mathbb{R}$ is the reward
function, and $\gamma \in (0,1)$ is the discount factor. The objective is to
find a policy $\pi: \mathcal{S}\to\Delta(\mathcal{A})$ maximising the
expected discounted return~\cite{levine2020offline}:
\begin{equation}
  J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\gamma^t r(s_t,a_t)\right].
  \label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
In the offline setting, the agent is given a fixed dataset
$\mathcal{D} = \{(s_i,a_i,r_i,s'_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ collected by a behaviour
policy $\pi_\beta$, with no further environment access. The Q-function
$Q^\pi(s,a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi[\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t r_t \mid s_0{=}s,
a_0{=}a]$ is estimated by minimising the Bellman residual on $\mathcal{D}$.

\subsection{Conservative Q-Learning}

CQL~\cite{kumar2020conservative} augments the Bellman objective with a
conservative regulariser:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CQL}}(\theta)
  &= \alpha\!\left(\mathbb{E}_{s\sim\mathcal{D},\,a\sim\mu}[Q_\theta(s,a)]
    - \mathbb{E}_{(s,a)\sim\mathcal{D}}[Q_\theta(s,a)]\right) \nonumber \\
  &\quad + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}}
    \!\left[\bigl(Q_\theta(s,a)-\hat{\mathcal{B}}^{\hat\pi}Q_{\bar\theta}(s,a)
    \bigr)^2\right],
  \label{eq:cql}
\end{align}
where $\mu$ is a policy that maximises $Q$, $\bar\theta$ are
target-network parameters, and $\alpha{>}0$ controls the degree of
conservatism. The softmax variant is used throughout. As noted
in~\cite{liu2023aptq}, the Q-value distribution varies substantially across
environments, making a single fixed $\alpha$ suboptimal in general.

\subsection{Epistemic Uncertainty via Q-Ensembles}

Following established practice~\cite{an2021pbrl,wu2021uncertainty}, epistemic
uncertainty is estimated through an ensemble of $K$ independently
initialised Q-networks $\{Q_{\theta_k}\}_{k=1}^K$:
\begin{equation}
  u(s,a) = \mathrm{std}\!\left(\{Q_{\theta_k}(s,a)\}_{k=1}^K\right).
  \label{eq:uncertainty}
\end{equation}
High ensemble disagreement proxies low data coverage at
$(s,a)$~\cite{an2021pbrl}. This formulation is used here both as a trained
baseline and, in the planned extension, as a diagnostic uncertainty map.

\subsection{Conservative and Pessimistic Offline RL}

CQL~\cite{kumar2020conservative} established conservative value estimation.
Batch-Constrained Q-learning~\cite{fujimoto2019bcq} pioneered
support-constrained policy learning. Pessimistic
RL~\cite{an2021pbrl} applies uncertainty-weighted penalties via
ensemble bootstraps. TD3-BC~\cite{fujimoto2021td3bc} adds a behavioural
cloning regulariser to a TD3-style actor-critic, providing a minimalist,
support-aware baseline. BEAR~\cite{kumar2019bear} enforces a support
constraint via kernel MMD between the learned policy and the behaviour
policy. Collectively these methods treat the pessimism degree as a critical
free parameter yet uniformly fix it as a scalar hyperparameter per task.

\subsection{Adaptive and Uncertainty-Aware Pessimism}

Adaptive Pessimism-based Target Q-learning
(APTQ)~\cite{liu2023aptq} adapts the CQL penalty via the moving-average
difference between expected Q-values and a dataset-quantile target.
Uncertainty-Weighted Actor-Critic~\cite{wu2021uncertainty} examines the
interaction between ensemble disagreement and policy-constraint strength.
Model-based Offline Policy Optimisation
(MOPO)~\cite{yu2020mopo} augments the offline dataset with short
model-generated rollouts under a reward penalty proportional to model
uncertainty. These works confirm that fixed pessimism is often
miscalibrated, yet their empirical focus remains predominantly on locomotion
tasks or aggregate global adaptation.

\subsection{Navigation Benchmarks}

Despite the dominance of locomotion benchmarks,
D4RL~\cite{fu2020d4rl} provides \texttt{maze2d} environments that expose
qualitatively distinct coverage and reward structures, which remain
underexplored from the perspective of pessimism calibration and form the
primary focus of this work. Implicit and advantage-weighted offline methods
represent a complementary class that mitigates overestimation without
explicit conservatism; integrating these as additional baselines is
identified as near-term future work.

\section{Empirical Framework}
\label{sec:method}

The framework comprises a unified training and evaluation protocol for
seven offline RL baselines on \texttt{maze2d-medium-sparse-v1}, and a
diagnostic toolkit---coverage-tier stratification together with the
Conservative Excess Ratio and Uncertainty--Return Correlation
metrics---designed to characterise \emph{where} and \emph{how severely}
conservatism is miscalibrated relative to epistemic uncertainty. Both the
baseline comparison (Section~\ref{sec:results}) and the tier diagnostics
(Section~\ref{sec:diagnostics}) are executed on the released dataset and
codebase.

\subsection{Baselines}

\textbf{TD3-BC}~\cite{fujimoto2021td3bc} augments a TD3 actor-critic update
with a behavioural-cloning regularisation term weighted by
$\lambda_{\mathrm{TD3}}$, anchoring the policy close to the data manifold.

\textbf{CQL}~\cite{kumar2020conservative} is implemented with the softmax
regulariser of Eq.~\eqref{eq:cql}, with $\alpha$ fixed uniformly throughout
training.

\textbf{Ensemble}: $K{=}5$ independently initialised critics are trained
with the standard Bellman objective only; the ensemble spread provides the
epistemic uncertainty signal of Eq.~\eqref{eq:uncertainty} and also serves
as an uncertainty-implicit baseline policy.

\textbf{BEAR}~\cite{kumar2019bear} constrains the learned policy to the
support of the behaviour policy via a kernel MMD penalty between sampled
actions and a pretrained conditional VAE of $\pi_\beta$.

\textbf{UWAC}~\cite{wu2021uncertainty} reweights actor-critic updates by the
inverse of ensemble disagreement, down-weighting updates in high-uncertainty
regions.

\textbf{MOPO}~\cite{yu2020mopo} learns a probabilistic dynamics model and
augments training with short synthetic rollouts, penalising the reward by
the dynamics model's predictive uncertainty.

\textbf{APTQ-CQL}~\cite{liu2023aptq} adapts a single global penalty
coefficient $\alpha_t$ at every training step via
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_t = \mathrm{clip}\!\bigl((1-\beta)\,\alpha_{t-1}
             + \beta\,\varphi_t,\;
             \alpha_{\min},\alpha_{\max}\bigr),
  \label{eq:aptq}
\end{equation}
where $\varphi_t$ is a reward/Q-deviation-derived signal and $\beta$ is the
adaptation rate. Unlike a coverage-conditioned scheme, $\alpha_t$ is shared
by all transitions at step $t$.

\subsection{Implementation Fidelity}

We implement educational reimplementations in PyTorch rather than fork
official codebases, so that all methods share identical network width
(256--256 MLPs), optimiser (Adam, lr $3{\times}10^{-4}$), batch size
(256), discount ($\gamma{=}0.99$), and training budget ($10$ outer
$\times$ $200$ inner gradient steps per seed). Actor-critic methods use
target critics with polyak averaging ($\tau{=}0.005$) and clipped
gradients (max norm $1.0$). This protocol prioritises \emph{controlled,
cross-method comparability} over reproducing published leaderboard scores;
results should be interpreted relative to other methods in
Table~\ref{tab:perf}, not as absolute D4RL benchmarks.

\subsection{Coverage-Tier Stratification}

The dataset $\mathcal{D}$ can be partitioned into tiers using a kNN
coverage-density proxy:
\begin{equation}
  \rho(s_i) = \frac{1}{d_k(s_i)},
  \label{eq:coverage}
\end{equation}
where $d_k(s_i)$ is the distance to the $k$-th nearest neighbour in
$\{s_j\}_{j\neq i}$, with $k{=}10$. Transitions are partitioned into
high, medium, and low coverage tertiles by $\rho(s_i)$.

\subsection{Miscalibration Diagnostic Metrics}

\textbf{Conservative Excess Ratio.} For coverage tier
$T \in \{\mathrm{High}, \mathrm{Med}, \mathrm{Low}\}$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CER}(T)=
  \frac{\mathbb{E}_{(s,a)\in T}[Q_{\mathrm{BC}}(s,a)-Q_{\mathrm{algo}}(s,a)]}
       {\mathbb{E}_{(s,a)\sim\mathcal{D}}[u(s,a)]}.
  \label{eq:cer}
\end{equation}
A high CER in the high-coverage tier would directly evidence
over-penalisation in well-supported regions that do not epistemically
warrant conservatism.

\textbf{Uncertainty--Return Correlation.} The Pearson correlation
between per-transition uncertainty and Monte Carlo return
$G_i = \sum_{t=i}^{T}\gamma^{t-i} r_t$ within tier $T$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{URC}(T)=
  \mathrm{Corr}\!\bigl(u(s_i,a_i),\,G_i\bigr)_{\!(s,a)\in T}.
  \label{eq:urc}
\end{equation}
Under good calibration, higher uncertainty should correspond to lower
returns, implying $\mathrm{URC}{<}0$; a near-zero or positive URC signals
miscalibration.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Dataset}

\texttt{maze2d-medium-sparse-v1}~\cite{fu2020d4rl} contains transitions
collected by a waypoint-following controller in a medium-complexity 2D maze.
The 4-dimensional state space encodes position and velocity, and the action
space is 2-dimensional and continuous. Coverage is dense along the
demonstrated path and near-zero in unvisited corridors; rewards are sparse
and goal-conditioned. This structure makes the dataset an appropriate
stress test for fixed-$\alpha$ conservatism: any single global penalty must
simultaneously suit both densely covered corridor states and sparsely
covered off-path states.

\subsection{Algorithms and Validation Protocol}

Seven algorithms are trained under a matched protocol: TD3-BC, CQL
($\alpha{=}1.0$), an uncertainty-aware Q-ensemble ($K{=}5$), BEAR, UWAC,
MOPO, and APTQ-CQL. Each algorithm is trained for three independent random
seeds ($\mathrm{seed}\in\{0,1,2\}$), with \texttt{torch}, \texttt{numpy},
and Python random seeds set per run. The reported score for each run is the
mean of the final three evaluation points of the training curve; the table
entry is the mean $\pm$ standard deviation across the three seeds.

\subsection{Evaluation Metric}

All algorithms share a single offline evaluation protocol. At each
training checkpoint, the policy $\pi$ and its critic(s) are frozen and the
score is the mean of $Q(s,\pi(s))$ over the first 5{,}000 transitions in
the dataset. The reported table entry is the mean of the final three
checkpoint scores, averaged over three random seeds. This
policy-value proxy enables fair cross-algorithm comparison under identical
training budgets, but it is \emph{not} a D4RL environment rollout or
normalised return~\cite{fu2020d4rl}. Dataset Monte Carlo returns have mean
$\approx 1.15$ and maximum $\approx 38$ on this split, which contextualises
the magnitude of reported $Q$ scores. Environment rollouts with official
D4RL normalisation are left to future work (MuJoCo/D4RL dependency).

\subsection{Diagnostic Protocol}

Coverage tiers are computed via $k$NN density (Eq.~\ref{eq:coverage},
$k{=}10$) on a stratified subsample of $5{\times}10^{4}$ transitions.
$Q_{\mathrm{BC}}$ is a critic regressed onto per-transition Monte Carlo
returns $G_i$; $u(s,a)$ is the standard deviation across a five-member
ensemble trained on the same targets. CER and URC (Eqs.~\ref{eq:cer}--\ref{eq:urc})
are reported per tier for CQL and APTQ-CQL (seed~0 checkpoints).

are both rare and locally heterogeneous---so $u(s,a)$ is a noisy calibration
signal without tier conditioning.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the results confirm}

The ranking in Table~\ref{tab:perf} separates explicit CQL-style critics
from lighter baselines, but tier diagnostics in Table~\ref{tab:diag} refine
the miscalibration story: a fixed global $\alpha$ does not over-penalise
well-covered corridors---it remains optimistic relative to $Q_{\mathrm{BC}}$
everywhere, with the largest optimism in low-coverage regions. This pattern
is more consistent with \emph{under}-penalisation of OOD value than with
over-penalisation along paths, and it motivates spatially conditioned
conservatism rather than a single scalar reduction of $\alpha$.

\subsection{From global to spatial adaptation}

APTQ's adaptation is \emph{temporal and global}: $\alpha_t$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:aptq} is shared by every transition at training step $t$. Its
second-place performance here shows that adaptive pessimism remains
competitive with fixed $\alpha$, but does not by itself establish that
global adaptation dominates a well-tuned fixed penalty on this benchmark.
The natural next experiment is to stratify transitions into coverage tiers
via Eq.~\eqref{eq:coverage}
and compute CER (Eq.~\eqref{eq:cer}) and URC (Eq.~\eqref{eq:urc}) for CQL,
APTQ-CQL, and a proposed Coverage-CQL variant that replaces the scalar
$\alpha$ with a per-sample penalty $\alpha(s) = f(\rho(s))$.

\subsection{Lower-tier baselines}

BEAR, TD3-BC, Ensemble, UWAC, and MOPO all score within a narrow band near
$0.01$--$0.02$ under the unified proxy. This clustering suggests that, at
the simplified training budget used here, support constraints and
uncertainty reweighting do not by themselves produce critics whose
policy-implied values match those of CQL-style methods on sparse maze
navigation data.

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Implementation scope.} Results use compact educational
reimplementations with a short training budget, not official author
codebases or tuned hyperparameters.

\textbf{Evaluation scope.} Proxy $Q$ scores are not D4RL normalised
returns; three seeds limit statistical power.

\textbf{Diagnostics scope.} CER/URC rely on ensemble uncertainty trained on
Monte Carlo targets and a single checkpoint per method; they indicate
calibration patterns but do not prove causal benefit of spatial $\alpha(s)$.

\textbf{Next steps.} (i)~D4RL rollouts with MuJoCo; (ii)~locomotion
datasets; (iii)~coverage-conditioned $\alpha(s){=}f(\rho(s))$ with tier-aware
evaluation; (iv)~implicit-Q and AWAC baselines.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper compares seven offline RL baselines on
\texttt{maze2d-medium-sparse-v1} under matched training and a unified
offline evaluation protocol, augmented with coverage-tier CER/URC
diagnostics. Fixed-$\alpha$ CQL achieves the highest proxy score but is
optimistic relative to a Monte Carlo value baseline in \emph{every}
coverage tier, especially where data are sparse; APTQ-CQL attenuates this
excess most strongly on well-covered paths. Positive URC across tiers shows
that ensemble uncertainty does not track return risk on this benchmark
without spatial conditioning. Together, these results argue for
coverage-aware pessimism $\alpha(s){=}f(\rho(s))$ as the next step, together
with environment-level evaluation on D4RL.
